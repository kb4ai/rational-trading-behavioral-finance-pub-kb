# Inaction as Action: Stoic Philosophy, Decision Theory, and the Ethics of Omission in Trading

[Collected 2026-03-13 | Claude Sonnet 4.6 via Claude Code]

---

## Abstract

The thesis that *inaction is a form of action* — that choosing not to act is itself a choice with full moral and financial weight — spans Stoic philosophy (prohairesis, apatheia), existentialist ontology (Sartrean radical freedom), Eastern philosophy (wu wei), formal decision theory (expected utility, regret theory), behavioral economics (omission bias, status quo bias), and practical finance (Buffett/Munger opportunity cost doctrine, Taleb's via negativa). This document synthesizes these threads into a unified framework for rational trading cognition. The empirical finding is robust: humans systematically under-weight omission costs relative to commission costs — a cognitive asymmetry that systematically misprices "hold" and "do nothing" decisions. Rational trading requires symmetric treatment of action and inaction under expected utility theory while simultaneously accounting for the pathological emotional asymmetry.

## 1. Stoic Philosophical Foundations

### 1.1 Prohairesis: The Architecture of Moral Choice

**Epictetus** (c. 50–135 CE) built his entire ethics on *prohairesis* (προαίρεσις) — the faculty of rational, deliberate choice. The term encompasses not merely acts of choosing but the capacity for choice itself. Crucially, prohairesis subsumes both action AND inaction:

> "Volition is by nature unimpeded" — *Discourses* 1.17.21

Epictetus's four things under our control are: opinion, impulse (ὁρμή), desire, and aversion (ἀφορμή). The positive impulse to act (hormê) and the negative impulse not to act (aphormê) are both expressions of prohairesis. The taxonomy is explicit in *Discourses* 3.2.1–2:

> "The second discipline concerns the impulse to act and not to act, and, in general, appropriate behaviour; so that he may act in an orderly manner and after due consideration, and not carelessly."

The trading implication is direct: the decision to hold a position, to refrain from rebalancing, to pass on a trade — these are each exercises of *aphormê* (negative impulse) and carry the same moral and rational weight as the corresponding commissions. There is no default "neutral" state. Every moment of holding is a renewed choice.

**Key Epictetan formulation on the asymmetry of materials vs. use:**

> "The materials of action are indifferent, but the use we make of them is not indifferent." — *Discourses* 2.5.1

> "Externals must be used with care, because their usage is not an indifferent matter, yet at the same time with composure and tranquility, because the material being used is indifferent." — *Discourses* 2.5.6

The ball-player analogy is instructive: the ball is intrinsically worthless, yet the player exerts full energy to catch it — *valuing the proper execution of action regardless of external stakes*. Applied to trading: the intrinsic value of any asset is subordinate to the quality of the decision process concerning it.

**Massimo Pigliucci** (K.D. Irani Professor, CCNY), in *How to Be a Stoic* (2017), frames prohairesis as the modern "dichotomy of control" and explicitly extends it to "decisions to act or not to act" as equally subject to rational scrutiny. The key passage: *only our considered judgments, explicitly endorsed values, and decisions to act or not to act are truly "up to us"* — making non-action a first-class citizen of the ethical will.

### 1.2 Apatheia: Active Rational Engagement, Not Passivity

A persistent misreading equates Stoic *apatheia* (ἀπάθεια) with passive withdrawal. The textual record is unambiguous: apatheia means the *absence of irrational passions* (pathê), not the absence of engagement.

**Marcus Aurelius**, *Meditations* 8.32:

> "It is thy duty to order thy life well in every single act; and if every act does its duty, as far as is possible, be content; and no one is able to hinder thee so that each act shall not do its duty."

On the injustice of inaction, *Meditations* 9.5 (widely attributed):

> "Often injustice is the result of inaction, not just of what you do."

Marcus Aurelius explicitly condemns disguising inaction as wisdom. On deliberate non-engagement as a choice requiring justification:

> From Alexander the Platonic: not frequently nor without necessity to say to any one, or to write in a letter, that I have no leisure; nor continually to *excuse the neglect of duties required by our relation to those with whom we live* by alleging urgent occupations. — *Meditations* 1 (on Alexander)

The phrase "excuse the neglect" frames inaction as something requiring affirmative justification, not as a safe default.

The Stoic "good emotions" (eupatheiai) that replace pathological passions include *boulêsis* (rational wishing) and *eulabia* (rational caution) — both active, directed states. Apatheia does not produce the withdrawn spectator but the *calm, rational agent* capable of vigorous action.

**Seneca**, *Epistulae Morales* (Letters to Lucilius):

* Letter 1: "The largest portion of our life passes while we are doing ill, a goodly share while we are doing nothing, and the while we are doing that which is not the purpose." — Time wasted through inaction is theft from oneself.
* Letter 55 (On Leisure vs. Idleness): "It makes a great deal of difference whether your life be one of leisure or one of idleness." — *Otium* (contemplative leisure) serves rational ends; mere idleness does not.
* Letter 78: "Only idle leisure is wont to make men hate their lives." — Unreasoned inaction produces psychological decay.

Seneca draws a distinction critical for trading: *strategic non-action chosen for positive reasons* (leisure, reflection, deliberate waiting) is categorically different from *drift, avoidance, or neglect*. The former is prohairesis in action; the latter is its failure.

### 1.3 Kathêkon: The Duty to Act Appropriately

Stoic *kathêkon* (καθήκον, appropriate action / "incumbent duty") creates positive moral obligations. Epictetus's three *topoi* (areas of training) require:

1. Correct desire/aversion
2. Correct impulse to act or not act
3. Correct reasoning

The second topos explicitly treats impulse to act and impulse not to act as symmetrically subject to correctness criteria. Failure to act when action is required (*kathêkon*) is as much a moral failure as acting when one ought not.

**For traders**: the Stoic framework generates a *fiduciary-like duty* to the rational management of one's portfolio. Failing to exit a loss-making position when the rational case is clear, or failing to enter when expected value clearly favors action, violates kathêkon regardless of whether it "feels" safe.

## 2. Philosophy of Action: The Omission-Commission Problem

### 2.1 Trolley Problem and the Doing-Allowing Distinction

**Philippa Foot** introduced the foundational scenarios in "The Problem of Abortion and the Doctrine of the Double Effect" (1967), distinguishing *doing harm* from *allowing harm*. The trolley problem (named by **Judith Jarvis Thomson**, 1985 essay "The Trolley Problem") crystallizes the action-inaction asymmetry:

* *Switch case*: divert trolley, kill 1, save 5 — most respondents (~90%) say permissible
* *Footbridge/fat man case*: push person off bridge, kill 1, save 5 — most respondents (~10%) say permissible

The asymmetry persists even with identical outcomes (1 death → 5 lives saved), revealing that the *mode of causation* (direct action vs. allowing/redirecting) creates strong moral intuitions independent of consequentialist calculus. The **Doctrine of Double Effect** (DDE), originating in Aquinas and elaborated by Foot, formalizes this: permissibility of harm depends on whether it is *intended* (means or end) vs. *foreseen but unintended* side-effect.

**Trading application**: DDE intuitions drive the psychological asymmetry where traders feel personally *responsible* for commission losses but merely *unfortunate* about omission losses. This is normatively unjustified but descriptively powerful.

### 2.2 Spranca, Minsk & Baron (1991) — Omission Bias Formalized

The landmark paper "Omission and commission in judgment and choice" (*Journal of Experimental Social Psychology* 27, pp. 76–105) provided the first systematic experimental account.

**Core design**: scenarios holding intentions, motives, and consequences constant, varying only whether harm resulted from action (commission) or inaction (omission).

**Key quantitative findings**:

| Experiment | N | Primary Finding |
|-----------|---|-----------------|
| 1 | 57 | 65–68% rated omissions as less immoral; mean diff = 27.6 pts (SD=21.2) on 100-pt scale |
| 2 | 36 | Bias persists with intention explicitly controlled; 10/33 showed bias despite rating intentions equal |
| 3 | 32 | 21/32 ranked harmful omission as better than equivalent commission (p<.001) |
| 4 | 48 | 16-pt mean difference favoring omission in self-affecting medical decisions (F₁,₄₆=10.2, p<.005) |

**Mechanisms identified** (importance-ranked by subjects):

1. **Perceived causality**: commissions assigned greater causal role (22/26 omission-bias subjects, χ²=13.26, p<.001)
2. **Presence**: outcome would occur if actor absent/ignorant (52% cited)
3. **Movement**: physical action involved (44% cited)
4. **Alternative cause**: other salient causes present in omissions (33–67% cited)

**Subject justification exemplifying the bias**:
> "If I do nothing, it's not exactly *me* who *did* it."
> "Choosing to do nothing isn't really immoral."
> "John [in omission] did not *recommend* the dressing."

**Authors' normative position** (Baron): when intentions, outcomes, and knowledge are held constant, omissions and commissions are morally equivalent. The bias is "an overgeneralization of a useful heuristic to cases in which it is not justified."

**2019 meta-analysis** (Yeung et al., *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*): confirmed omission-commission asymmetry across morality and blame ratings, linked to causality and intentionality attribution patterns — robust across 30 years of replication.

### 2.3 Sartre: The Ontological Impossibility of Non-Choice

**Jean-Paul Sartre**, *Being and Nothingness* (1943) and *Existentialism is a Humanism* (1945):

> "Man is condemned to be free. Condemned, because he did not create himself, yet, in other respects is free; because, once thrown into the world, he is responsible for everything he does."

Sartre's ontology of the *pour-soi* (for-itself) establishes that consciousness is *always already* choosing. The attempt to "not choose" is itself a choice — specifically, the choice of *bad faith* (*mauvaise foi*). Bad faith is the self-deceptive denial of one's freedom by attributing one's situation to external necessity.

The waiter example (*Being and Nothingness*, Part 1, Ch. 2): the waiter who performs his role with excessive exactness, pretending he has no alternative, is in bad faith — he denies his freedom to quit, to act differently.

**Trading analogue**: the trader who holds a losing position "because I haven't decided to sell" is in Sartrean bad faith. The failure to sell is a daily renewed decision to continue holding. The passive framing conceals an active choice.

Sartre's concept of *facticity* vs. *transcendence* maps onto the trading situation: facticity is the given state of the portfolio; transcendence is the ongoing capacity to change it. Bad faith consists in collapsing transcendence into facticity — treating the current portfolio as if it were simply "what is" rather than "what I continuously choose to maintain."

### 2.4 Wu Wei: Active Non-Action vs. Passive Neglect

**Laozi**, *Tao Te Ching* (c. 4th century BCE): *wu wei* (無為) — "non-action" or "effortless action" — is systematically misread as passive withdrawal. The critical distinction:

* **Wu wei**: deliberate, aware, minimal intervention aligned with natural flow. The sage *acts through not forcing*. It is "actionless action" — full engagement with minimal friction.
* **Passive neglect**: unconscious drift, avoidance, inertia, failure to track conditions.

The *Tao Te Ching* Ch. 48: "In pursuit of learning, every day something is acquired. In pursuit of Tao, every day something is dropped." — wu wei is *active subtraction*, not passive accumulation of inaction.

**The critical difference for trading**: wu wei describes the master trader who *chooses not to trade* after thorough evaluation — a high-information, low-action state. Passive neglect describes the trader who doesn't review positions because it produces anxiety. The former is strategic non-action; the latter is omission bias in action.

This distinction maps onto **Nassim Taleb's** distinction between *fragile* inaction (avoiding decisions out of fear, deferring indefinitely) and *antifragile* strategic positioning (deliberate optionality preservation through inaction).

## 3. Decision Theory: Formal Treatment of Inaction

### 3.1 Expected Utility Theory: Symmetric Treatment

**Von Neumann-Morgenstern** expected utility theory (1944) treats action and inaction symmetrically: "hold" is simply one action in the feasible action set A = {buy, sell, hold, short, ...}, evaluated identically via EU(a) = Σ p(s) · u(outcome(a,s)). There is no privileged status for the status quo. Any preference for "hold" beyond its EU-justified level is, by construction, irrational.

**Savage's** subjective expected utility (1954) extends this to subjective probabilities, but maintains full symmetry: the decision not to act is evaluated against all other feasible decisions under the same subjective probability measure.

**The formal irrationality**: if EU(sell) > EU(hold) given one's beliefs and preferences, holding is EU-suboptimal regardless of whether "sell" involves physical action. The procedural distinction (action vs. inaction) has no standing in EU theory.

### 3.2 Regret Theory: Loomes & Sugden (1982)

**Graham Loomes and Robert Sugden**, "Regret Theory: An Alternative Theory of Rational Choice under Uncertainty" (*Economic Journal* 92(368), pp. 805–824, 1982) introduced a utility function incorporating anticipated regret:

Modified utility: **M(xᵢ, xⱼ) = u(xᵢ) + R(u(xᵢ) - u(xⱼ))**

where R(·) is a regret-rejoice function (R > 0 for rejoicing, R < 0 for regret), xᵢ is the chosen outcome, xⱼ is the forgone outcome.

**Key property**: R(·) is strictly increasing and R(-y) = -R(y) — regret and rejoicing are not symmetric in magnitude (regret tends to dominate). This formalizes loss aversion within a comparative framework.

**Omission-commission asymmetry in regret theory**: anticipated regret depends on *counterfactual salience*. After a commission loss, the forgone alternative (not acting) is maximally salient — "I did this; had I not, I would have been better off." After an omission loss, the forgone action is less psychologically salient (inaction feels less agentic). This *asymmetric counterfactual availability* drives the empirical finding that regret of commission exceeds regret of omission at equivalent outcome magnitudes.

**Kahneman & Miller (1986)** "Norm Theory": affective response to an event is enhanced if its causes are *abnormal* (unexpected, unusual). Commissions are abnormal (the default is inaction), so the same loss magnitude produces more regret when caused by action than by inaction. This is a *reference point asymmetry*, not an outcome asymmetry.

**Empirical calibration** (Ritov & Baron, 1990): subjects were willing to accept *greater expected loss* from omission than commission — specifically, tolerating 20% risk of harm through inaction vs. 15% risk through action. The omission premium in risk tolerance was ~33% in this domain.

### 3.3 Samuelson & Zeckhauser (1988): Status Quo Bias

**William Samuelson and Richard Zeckhauser**, "Status Quo Bias in Decision Making" (*Journal of Risk and Uncertainty* 1(1), pp. 7–59, 1988) provided the foundational behavioral economics treatment.

**Definition**: status quo bias is a disproportionate preference for maintaining the current state, beyond what rational expected utility maximization would predict.

**Key finding**: across experimental and field data (Harvard faculty health plan selections, retirement program enrollment), subjects disproportionately stuck with default options. When a new option was framed as the status quo, it gained a statistical advantage of ~10–20 percentage points in selection probability.

**Mechanisms** (Samuelson & Zeckhauser's taxonomy):

1. *Rational* explanations: transition costs, risk aversion, information costs
2. *Psychological* explanations: loss aversion (losses from change loom larger than gains), regret aversion, anchoring/adjustment, cognitive dissonance

**Baron's disentanglement** (Status-quo and omission biases, *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty*, with follow-up paper): across three experiments, omission bias proved *more powerful* than status quo preference per se. In Experiment 2, subjects showed conflicting choices favoring inaction in 72% of cases where the action factor varied, but only 51% favoring status quo when state-change varied independently (p<.001). The primary driver is aversion to *agency* in harmful outcomes, not attachment to current state.

**Formal decomposition**:

Let SQB = Status Quo Bias, OB = Omission Bias, SCB = Status Change Bias.

SQB ≈ OB + SCB, but empirically OB >> SCB. The "status quo" preference is largely an artifact of the typical confounding of "maintaining current state" with "taking no action."

### 3.4 Bostrom & Ord (2006): The Reversal Test

**Nick Bostrom and Toby Ord**, "The Reversal Test: Eliminating Status Quo Bias in Applied Ethics" (*Ethics* 116(4), pp. 656–679, 2006; preprint: nickbostrom.com/ethics/statusquo.pdf):

**Definition**: status quo bias is *an inappropriate (irrational) preference for an option because it preserves the status quo* — distinct from rational preferences for stability, predictability, or continuity.

**The Reversal Test heuristic**: if parameter P is currently at value V, and you oppose changing V to V' (higher), ask: would you also oppose changing V' to V (lower) if V' were the status quo? If yes to both, the onus is on you to explain why V is uniquely optimal. If no explanation is forthcoming, status quo bias is the likely culprit.

**Formal criterion**: status quo bias is present when *asymmetric resistance to change* cannot be justified by differential transition costs, risk profiles, or genuine optima at the current value.

**Trading application (Reversal Test)**:

> "I don't want to sell this position because it will lock in a loss."

Reversal: "If I did not own this position, would I buy it at the current price?"

If No, then holding it is status quo bias. The *only rational reason* to hold a position is the same reason one would buy it de novo at the current price, adjusted for transaction costs. Any other reason for holding is irrational privileging of the status quo.

## 4. Applications to Trading and Investing

### 4.1 Buffett & Munger: Opportunity Cost as First-Class Obligation

**Warren Buffett** has repeatedly framed omission costs as equal to or exceeding commission costs:

* **"Thumb-sucking"**: Buffett's term for being paralyzed by indecision when an opportunity clearly within one's circle of competence presents itself. In the 1994 Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting, Buffett estimated his failure to act on Fannie Mae during a period of distress cost Berkshire *at least $5 billion* — a paradigm case of omission cost exceeding any plausible commission risk.
* 1989 Berkshire Annual Letter: "Our biggest mistakes have been *mistakes of omission* — cases where we saw the right thing to do but failed to do it."

**Charlie Munger** on opportunity cost as the primary filter:

> "I would argue that one filter that's useful in investing is the simple idea of opportunity cost... The right way to think about investing is to [think] about your best opportunity cost." — *Poor Charlie's Almanack*

> "Warren's way of having every dollar retained having to produce at least a dollar of market value is the best way to describe our cost of capital." — Berkshire AGM (multiple iterations)

Munger's formulation *operationalizes* inaction as action: every dollar of cash held is an *active investment* in cash at the risk-free rate, benchmarked against the next-best idea. Holding cash is not "doing nothing" — it is choosing cash over alternatives.

**Seth Klarman** (*Margin of Safety*, 1991, out-of-print but widely cited): frames inaction as a positive choice with the opportunity cost of cash compounding. "The risk of paying too much for good quality assets is smaller than the risk of paying fair prices for poor quality assets" — but both involve *active choice*, not passive default.

**Structural reformulation**: in the Buffett-Munger framework, the portfolio state at any time T is the result of a sequence of *active choices*, with "hold" being a specific action chosen over all alternatives. There is no passive state.

### 4.2 Taleb: Via Negativa and the Asymmetry of Inaction

**Nassim Nicholas Taleb**, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (2012), Ch. 19 "The Philosopher's Stone and Its Inverse":

**Via negativa** (from negative theology): defining what something *is not* generates more robust knowledge than positive assertion. Applied to action: *what not to do* (iatrogenics avoided, harm prevented) is more reliably valuable than *what to do* (positive prescriptions).

> "Negative knowledge grows more robustly than positive knowledge."

**Asymmetric payoff structure of inaction**: Taleb distinguishes between two types of inaction:

1. *Fragile inaction*: holding a position that has negative convexity (more to lose than gain from further moves). This is the *sucking your thumb* failure mode.
2. *Antifragile inaction*: preserving optionality by not committing. This is *strategic non-action* — wu wei applied to finance.

**The Barbell Strategy** is the formal implementation: extreme safety (T-bills, cash) + extreme speculation (deep OTM options, venture bets). The middle (conventional bond-equity portfolio) is "medium fragility." The "hold nothing/hold extremes" strategy involves *deliberate inaction in the middle of the distribution*.

**Iatrogenics in trading** (*Antifragile*, Ch. 22): the most common error is *acting when one should not*. Medical parallel: most iatrogenic harm comes from interventions that would not have been made had the baseline rate of non-intervention been the default. Taleb explicitly frames over-trading as financial iatrogenics.

**Formula**: for any action with nonlinear payoffs, *expected harm from intervention* must clear a bar proportional to the convexity of the harm function. Small negative convexity = high intervention threshold. High positive convexity = low intervention threshold.

### 4.3 Quantitative Finance: The Hold Decision as Active Choice

**Opportunity cost of inaction** — The NBER working paper "The Opportunity Cost of Inaction in Financial Markets" (Jain, 2008, NBER-MMF08) models the case that cash-equivalent inaction is priced against the full opportunity set.

**Standard portfolio theory formalization**: in any period t, the "do nothing" decision (δ=0) has the same mathematical status as any other δ ∈ ℝⁿ. The expected return of holding is:

E[R_hold] = Σᵢ wᵢ · E[Rᵢ] — this is an *active* return expectation, not a null state.

**Active vs. passive management literature**: even "passive" index investment is an active choice of benchmark, rebalancing frequency, and factor exposures. **Brinson, Hood & Beebower (1986)** and subsequent analysis shows asset allocation (the *structure* of inaction between rebalancing events) explains ~90% of portfolio return variance — i.e., the "hold" decision dominates execution decisions in long-run performance.

**Rebalancing as the frame**: any portfolio unrebalanced is a portfolio *continuously drifting* toward its overperforming assets. "Not rebalancing" is an active choice to momentum-tilt. Vanguard analysis (Wallick et al.) finds unrebalanced portfolios drift from 60/40 equity/bond to ~70/30+ within 5 years without active rebalancing — inaction has a quantifiable consequence vector.

## 5. Synthesis: The Unified Framework

### 5.1 The Symmetry Principle

Across all four frameworks, a single normative conclusion emerges:

**Action (commission) and inaction (omission) are symmetrically subject to rational evaluation under any coherent decision theory.**

The asymmetry between them is *descriptive* (how humans actually feel) not *normative* (how they should reason). Specifically:

| Domain | Symmetry Claim | Asymmetry Finding |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Stoic prohairesis | Hormê and aphormê are both expressions of rational will | Culturally, inaction is coded as "safe" default |
| EU theory | Hold ∈ feasible action set, evaluated identically | Status quo premium ~10–20pp beyond EU-justified |
| Regret theory | Anticipated regret should be computed symmetrically | Commission regret > omission regret at equal outcomes |
| Omission bias | No moral difference given equal intentions/outcomes | 65–68% subjects rate commissions as worse |
| Finance practice | Opportunity cost applies to all options including cash | "Lost" opportunity costs cognitively discounted |

### 5.2 The Three Failure Modes of Inaction

1. **Omission bias** (Spranca et al.): judging inaction as less blameworthy than equivalent commission → systematically under-penalizes bad "hold" decisions.

2. **Status quo bias** (Samuelson & Zeckhauser): treating current portfolio state as privileged → failure to exit underperforming positions, failure to rebalance, failure to act on deteriorating thesis.

3. **Thumb-sucking / regret aversion** (Buffett/Kahneman): paralysis on good opportunities within competence from fear of commission errors → asymmetric regret structure inflates omission costs after the fact but suppresses action before the fact.

### 5.3 The Reversal Test as Trading Heuristic

Operationalizing Bostrom & Ord for portfolio decisions:

**For any held position**: "If I did not own this position today, would I buy it at the current price, size, and risk parameters?"

* Yes → holding is justified
* No → holding is status quo bias; the appropriate action is to exit
* Uncertain → this uncertainty requires the same investigation that would precede initiating a new position

**For any avoided action**: "If I had already done this and needed to decide whether to reverse it, would I reverse it?"

* No → the action is probably justified; avoiding it is omission bias
* Yes → genuine reasons against action exist

### 5.4 Stoic Decision Protocol for "Inaction" Decisions

Drawing on Epictetan screening of impressions (*Discourses* 2.18.24: "Impression, wait for me a little. Let me see what you are"):

1. **Surface the choice explicitly**: name the inaction as a choice. "I am choosing to hold X" not "I have X."
2. **Apply symmetric scrutiny**: subject the hold decision to the same analytical rigor as a new entry.
3. **Distinguish apatheia from passivity**: is this non-action a reasoned *aphormê* or an avoidance of emotional discomfort?
4. **Apply the reversal test**: would I initiate this position de novo?
5. **Check for bad faith** (Sartrean): am I claiming "I haven't decided" when in fact I am deciding continuously by not acting?
6. **Compute opportunity cost** (Munger): what is the true cost of capital, benchmarked against next-best alternative?
